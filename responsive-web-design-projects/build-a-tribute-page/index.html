1920 - March 14

Vitka is born in Kalisz (Kalisch), Poland.

1939 - September

Germans invade Poland. Vitka flees with her brother. She leaves him with their grandparents and goes to Vilna, Lithuania.

1941 - June

Germans occupy Vilna and force over 20,000 (exact number is disputed widely) Jews into a tiny ghetto.

1941 - July-November

At some point in this time-frame, Vitka met Rozka Korczak, whom she would befriend. Together they joined Ha-Shomer Ha-Tsa'ir, a Zionist youth group led by Abba Kovner.

1941 - December

Abba and Jacob Gens (the camp leader) learn from a survivor that Jews that are taken "East" are getting killed in pits. Jacob doesn't believe the survivor and thinks they are crazy. He and the Jewish police decide no one should hear the survivor's story.

1941 - December 31

Abba delivers a now-famous speech to Ha-Shomer Ha-Tsa'ir (this is the speech that uses the famous phrase, "We will not be led like sheep to the slaughter"). Ha-Shomer Ha-Tsa'ir begins organizing a resistance. Vitka begins searching for places to retaliate against the Germans.

1942 - January 21

United Partisan Organization (UPO, a.k.a. Fareynigte Partizaner Organizatsye, FPO) was founded as an organized resistance effort by Vitka, Abba, and others.

1942 - June 8

Vitka and a group of the resistance blow up a German train, thought to be one of the first acts of sabotage in occupied Europe.

1943 - July

Gens betrays FPO by surrendering their commander Yitzhak Wittenberg to the Germans. The resistance rescued Wittenberg, but the Germans told the Jews that if they didn't give him back, they would kill every Jew. This led to a full day of fighting amongst the Jews while Vitka hid Wittenberg. In the end Wittenberg was handed over and he committed suicide in jail.

1943 - July-August

The resistance began abandoning the ghetto to fight in the woods. Vitka would escort them out in waves. She'd take them to a Jewish cemetery first where she'd buried weapons previously so they could dig them up and use them when they were fleeing. Before they could all escape, the Nazis launched a raid and rounded up thousands to send to work camps. Resistance groups got ready for battle and reported to two locations while they awaited weapons.

1943 - September 1

Gens betrayed one of the resistance's meeting points, which led to many of them going down fighting. Vitka and Rozka almost died in the fighting but managed to escape through the sewers with a small group of survivors.

1943 - September - 1944 - June

Many things happened in this time period including:

  • The Jewish resistance formed into Ha-Nokmim, which translates to "The Avengers".
  • Vitka became commander of a patrol group that collected information, maintained contact with the underground in Vilna, transferred medications, led combat missions, and performed acts of sabotage. She also led groups of Jewish fighters to the forest camp until they numbered six hundred.
  • During this time, Vitka and Abba began their personal relationship.
  • Vitka led an attack on the Vilna electric grid while another group attacked the sewers. When they finished, they met up. Part of the group wanted to sleep in the city, but Kempner and others decided to leave. Those that stayed behind, were caught.
  • The Jewish partisans had to resort to raiding local peasantry to get enough food and supplies. Through it all, Vitka refused to take for her own well-being. The Russians would laugh at her and say she was not a human being, since she seemed to not need food or sleep.

1944 - July

Vitka led a patrol unit to help Jewish Soviet solders to take back Vilna. She was awarded the highest badge of courage in the USSR. Many others in the group, including Rozka, also earned medals, which afforded them some mobility in the now-Russian occupied Vilna.

1945 - January

The Soviet secret police began arresting dissidents, which included Abba's and Vitka's group. They decided to send Rozka to British Palestine to gain support for an exodus of Jews from Europe to Palestine.

1945 - Passover

Abba forms a new group with Vitka called Nakam, which means revenge. Its members felt they had no right to move on with their lives and that those responsible for the Holocaust should suffer. They did not believe there would be justice in international courts. They planned to poison the waterworks of some of the most important cities to the Nazis, indiscriminately killing millions of Germans.

1945 - Fall

Abba had Rozka introduce him to people who could help with their efforts in seeking revenge, under the pretense of "justice". He did not usually state their goal as the mass killings, and rather as more targeted attacks.

1946 - January

Abba was arrested on his way back to Europe and sent one last letter to Vitka telling her to enact plan B: poisoning Nazi POWs.

1946 - April 13

Nakam infiltrated a bakery supplying bread for POWs at Nuremberg. They poisoned ~2300 loaves with arsenic before they had to escape. According to news reports, thousands fell ill, but none died.

1946 - July

Abba, Rozka, and Vitka re-unite in Palestine, along with the Nakam members. The group engaged in arguments about ending their operations and starting a new life. Abba, Rozka, and Vitka all wanted the war to end and to start anew.

1946 - August-December

Vitka and Abba marry and settle down in Palestine. Rozka ends up living 20 yards away.

1948

Vitka gives birth to her and Abba's first child, Michael.

1951 - 1953

Vitka contracted tuberculosis. Her enforced distance from Michael caused her a lot of suffering during this time. To cope, Vitka began taking university courses via correspondence. She studied history, English, and French.

1956

Vitka gave birth to her second child, Shlomit. Due to her previous bout with tuberculosis, she was prohibited from most forms of contact with Shlomit, which caused more suffering for Vitka.

1965-1968

Vitka began studying clinical psychology full time. Var Ilan University let her complete her studies for both her first and second degrees within 3 years. While studying, Vitka developed a new form of therapy, "Non-verbal therapy by color." It proved very successful in the Unit for Parent-Child Treatment at Oranim Teachers' College.

1968 - 1985

Vitka supported Abba through his endeavors as a poet, leader, philosopher, and visionary.

1985 - Summer

Abba got diagnosed with cancer.

1987 - September 25

Abba passed away.

1988 - March 5

Rozka passed away from cancer.

1988 - 2012

Throughout this time, Vitka's heroism and struggles throughout the partisan movement became more common knowledge. Vitka continued working as a psychologist for her community for the rest of her life.

2012 - February

Vitka passed away.
